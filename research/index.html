Current Research

Imagine you are kayaking on a river. You have become stuck in a section above a massive waterfall. Going over means death. The current is fickle–because it sometimes overwhelms even maximum paddling, survival is not guaranteed. Finding the optimal trade-off between risk and effort requires us to uncover the implied value in avoiding disaster. I have developed an approach–shadow value viability–which prescribes when you should be paddling and how far upstream you should aim to be, in order to ensure a desirable degree of safety.

My primary research focus has been to better understand the implied value of threshold avoidance goals and translate this value into the benefits of reducing risk on the margin. A value that changes as the risk of crossing becomes more or less salient is exactly the missing demand curve we need to convert the cost-effective avoidance problem into one of maximizing net benefits, which brings in all of the past intuition and guidance that resource economics has to offer.

Balancing conservation and commerce

With Michael Springborn.

This paper develops a theory of the non-use value–tied to a change in the risk of extinction–of individuals in an endangered population, and provides an explanation of why the objective of ensuring ongoing species viability is a technically-challenging one. Fortunately, we provide a solution for solving this type of problem. Because contributions come in threes, we use our newfound species value to produce market-based instruments to aid the integrated management of the Pacific swordfish fishery and the long-term survival of the Pacific leatherback turtle.

Keywords: viable control, dynamic programming methods, shadow valuation, conservation, catastrophes, bioeconomics, bycatch, market-based instruments, multi-species fisheries management

Efficient avoidance of tipping points

I’m searching for an alternative to DICE-like models for carbon pricing and emissions planning. My current approach maximizes a metric of [reversible] global warming potential (energy absorbed by the Earth) while constraining the likelihood of irreversible climate change to some maximal tolerable level. This objective matches the spirit of the Paris Agreement goals and removes the need for numerous unfalsifiable assumptions about the global economy. The “value function” being maximized yields our threshold avoidance budget–the expected amount of remaining warming potential that doesn’t induce irreversible warming. A viable cost of carbon–a physical marginal user cost–provides the opportunity cost of sparing more of our budget for additional emissions in the future.

Keywords: viable cost of carbon, tipping points, climate change, approximate dynamic programming

Integrated ecosystem management and hydropower generation in endangered species recovery

With Lucas Bair, Matthew Reimer, and Michael Springborn.

Non-native rainbow trout are now the dominant fish species in Glen Canyon and Marble Canyon, the stretches of river directly below Glen Canyon Dam. Dam operation is thought to have negatively affected humpback chub abundances downstream via decreased water temperature and turbidity, conditions that have also improved rainbow trout numbers. Trout management flows–large dam releases designed to strand younger, more vulnerable trout–can improve conditions for humpback chub. However, hydropower revenues will be reduced. This study examines this tradeoff and synergies with other trout management actions, such as electrofishing, while expanding on the bioeconomic model found in our previous work.

Keywords: trout management flows, hydropower, non-native species control
