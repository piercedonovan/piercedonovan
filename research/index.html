Current Research

A general class of problems that has received relatively little attention in economics is concerned with staying above (or below) a particular threshold over time with a specified margin of safety. Notable examples include maintaining endangered species, global temperature increases, and diseases safely away from extinction, disaster and outbreak thresholds, respectively. These examples share two key attributes which make the application of standard expected net benefit objectives problematic: dynamic uncertainty is central, and the consequences of crossing the threshold are dire and/or hard to estimate. In these cases, we can substitute a “chance constraint” in lieu of some “benefits” function (as is normally prescribed) and seek to minimize the cost of compliance. Thus the trade-off of interest is between the cost of action that forestalls disaster and the reduction in risk associated with that action.

My current research develops analytical and computational methods to solve these types of problems. Some of the key features of these problems prohibit the usual set of tools available to most economists, and my aim is to not only bring these types of objectives to light, but also fill in the methodological gaps that make these types of problems difficult.

Below, you’ll find summaries on three of my current research projects. Feel free to contact me with any comments and questions. I also welcome any and all opportunities for collaboration.

A practical, viability-based program for optimal bycatch management and valuation

Fishing quota programs continue to replace command-and-control policies in commercial fisheries management, due to their past successes in improving the value and sustainability of many fisheries. The scope of the market policy instrument includes ecological objectives as well, as the introduction of markets for non-targeted, ecologically-important species have successfully limited bycatch.

In a market, a price signal is the only stimulus needed to efficiently coordinate the actions of the individual participants. In a fisheries context, this price is informed by the scarcity of quota, set by the total allowable catch (TAC). Given a TAC, a competitive market will reach an optimal state, with quota flowing to those who value it the most. In the case of bycatch, the TAC will be met at a minimum welfare loss. TACs are often chosen to favor a certain level of stock health over maximizing fisheries rents (or minimizing fishermen’s welfare loss), and the methods for choosing the TAC are often ad-hoc and fail to reflect the ecological value of the bycatch species. Thus the market instrument introduces a lot of complicated machinery without gaining the full potential payoff, due to less-than-optimal management.

I aim to describe a practical management alternative, one that requires much less infrastructure than a bycatch quota system. My method, similar in style to that of Safety in numbers (see publications tab), can inform the viability-consistent landings price that leverages the implied value of a conservation objective against the potential rents of a fishery. Alternatively, as many fisheries shut down once any species TAC is met, this method can derive the optimal quantity restriction on bycatch instead. This method provides a new, pragmatic option for bycatch management and expands our understanding of the implict valuation of ecological goals.

A safety-first model of avoiding climate catastrophe

The Paris Agreement seeks to limit average global temperature rise to two degrees celcius in the present century, compared to pre-industrial levels. This threshold is seen as the point beyond which risk of climate catastrophe greatly increases. Since the beginning of the modern climate change discussion, the focus of the central planner has been to manage “emissions” in maximizing a measure of expected welfare. However, the phrasing of the problem is more accurately identified as a safety-first-type objective.

I am working on a continuous dynamic programming problem that compares the optimal feedback policies of the safety-first and welfare-oriented objectives. I incorporate uncertainty in dynamics using the stochastic calculus of Ito, and track the likelihood of violating the above threshold with the distribution of the running maximum temperature anomaly. Because this problem can be solved analytically, I plan to glean a deeper understanding of the shadow value concept described in Safety in numbers.

Analysis of the Pacific halibut charter fishery in Alaska

With Dan Lew and Jim Sanchirico

Pacific halibut is one of the primary saltwater sport fish targeted by recreational anglers in Alaska. In the two decades before 2011, the recreational saltwater charter fishery in Alaska had undergone considerable growth. Despite this, the extent to which the charter fishing sector have been impacted by charter-specific harvest regulations and other changes in the fishery resulting from the implementation of the Halibut Catch Sharing Plan (a limited entry program) in 2014, such as the introduction of a guided angler fish (quota leasing) program, have not been carefully analyzed.

This research seeks to investigate the factors influencing decisions by participants in the recreational charter halibut fishery, using a unique dataset cataloging charter fishing effort. Specifically, we will develop econometric models of charter businesses to estimate (1) the season-level decision of whether to exit or stay in the recreational charter halibut fishery, (2) intra-seasonal trip-taking behavior by recreational charter businesses, and (3) whether a particular operation chooses to participate in the quota market.
