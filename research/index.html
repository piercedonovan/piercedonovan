Current Research

Imagine you are kayaking on a river. You have become stuck in a section above a massive waterfall. Going over means death. The current is fickle–because it sometimes overwhelms even maximum paddling, survival is not guaranteed. Finding the optimal trade-off between risk and effort requires us to uncover the implied value in avoiding disaster. I have developed an approach–Shadow Value Viability–that does just this, which prescribes when you should be paddling and how far upstream you should aim to be, in order to ensure a desirable degree of safety.

My primary research focus is designing and analyzing practical approaches for addressing risk and uncertainty in natural resource management. I have concentrated on management settings in which the standard net benefit maximization cannot be applied due to a lack of reasonable estimates of the values of key events – like species extinction or climate catastrophes.

Balancing conservation and commerce: A shadow value viability approach for handling bycatch in a multi-species fishery (JMP)

The benefits from avoiding [irreversible] extinction events are often not well-known, which makes an expected net benefits approach to conservation problems difficult to justify. A viable control strategy instead focuses on limiting the risk of extinction to some acceptable level. Here I extend a recently developed shadow value viability approach for solving conservation problems with dynamic programming. As a social planner problem, the method involves identifying the loss from extinction that drives enough conservation effort to ensure survival with a given confidence. In my numerical application, the Pacific leatherback turtle population co-mingles with the California drift gillnet swordfish fishery and the risk of bycatch threatens both turtle survival and swordfish revenues. Using the social [fishery] planner’s shadow value of leatherback extinction, I set market-based instruments for managing turtle bycatch that incentivize socially-optimal fishing behavior among decentralized fishers. This approach translates the viability objective into economic terms so that conservation and commercial harvest can be rationally integrated.

Keywords: viable control, dynamic programming methods, shadow valuation, conservation, catastrophes, bioeconomics, bycatch, market-based instruments, multi-species fisheries management

A viability cost of carbon (Dissertation Chapter)

This is my modeling response to popular concerns with integrated assessment modeling. Viable control, in this context, replaces the maximization of intergenerational utility with a probabilistic climate constraint (such as limiting the likelihood of crossing the 2°C threshold outlined in the Paris Agreement to 15%). This relieves results from their dependence on assumptions about key “policy” parameters and loss functions, and importantly places focus on preventing “tail events” (the language suggested by scientists and used in policy). The shadow value of crossing the 2°C threshold – the marginal expected present damage due to disaster – yields the viability cost of carbon, an alternative social damage measure consistent with this viability goal.

Keywords: viability cost of carbon, VCC, climate change, fat tails

Integrated ecosystem management and hydropower generation in endangered species recovery

With Lucas Bair, Matthew Reimer, and Michael Springborn.

Non-native rainbow trout are now the dominant fish species in Glen Canyon and Marble Canyon, the stretches of river directly below Glen Canyon Dam. Dam operation is thought to have negatively affected humpback chub abundances downstream via decreased water temperature and turbidity, conditions that have also improved rainbow trout numbers. Trout management flows – large dam releases designed to strand younger, more vulnerable trout – can improve conditions for humpback chub. However, hydropower revenues will be reduced. This study examines this tradeoff and synergies with other trout management actions, such as electrofishing, while expanding on the bioeconomic model found in Safety in Numbers.

Keywords: trout management flows, hydropower, non-native species control

Exit behavior and the nature of trip taking in the South Alaska charter fishery

With Daniel Lew and James Sanchirico

The guided recreational saltwater charter fishery in Alaska has been exposed to new harvest regulations, limited entry programs, and quota leasing systems over the past 10 years, in response to calls for more sustainable management. Charter business owners have expressed concerns that these policies have increased privatization and concentration of the industry. We are studying trip-taking behavior and the seasonal entry-exit decision with charter logbook data to verify the predicted response to changes in the political seascape.

Keywords: charter fishing, entry-exit, policy evaluation

Limited foresight: A rational response to an ambiguous future

An interesting behavior exhibited by politicians, CEOs, and natural resource managers is the limiting of a planning horizon in response to an ambiguous future. In cases of deep uncertainty, there may be a grand horizon over which one wishes to optimize, but only a short horizon where any calculus is truly conceivable; this confines their optimization problem within a rolling window and produces drastically different prescriptions of “optimal behavior.” I believe this solution concept is particularly apt to tackle problems with dynamic systems that are expected to change over time due to climate change.

Keywords: bounded rationality, ambiguity aversion, limited foresight
