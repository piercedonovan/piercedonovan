Current Research

My current research “grail” is to understand the behavior of resource managers facing ecological objectives amid uncertainty. This type of objective departs from the classical welfare maximization schema namely because it responds to a physical constraint rather than some estimated expected future benefit. I develop analytical and computational solution concepts that solve this type of problem. Some of the key features of these objectives prohibit the usual set of tools available to most economists, and my aim is to not only bring these types of objectives to light, but also fill in the methodological gaps that make these types of problems difficult.

Below, you’ll find summaries on some of my current research projects. Feel free to contact me with any comments and questions. I’d appreciate any opportunities for collaboration.

Bycatch management and valuation with viability-consistent market instruments

In fisheries management, an enforceable total allowable catch facilitates the rebuilding of fish stocks, including those of bycatch species characterized by ecological importance or existence values. In multispecies fisheries, this creates a new problem: fisheries must close once the limit for any one species is met. Hitting the limit of a vulnerable, non-targeted species will leave “money in the ocean,” as a healthy stock of the targeted species can no longer be harvested. I use the tradeoff between a long-run survival goal for a bycatch species and the potential rents of a commercial fishery to provide a recommendation for a landings price on bycatch that eliminates the need for premature closures. This avoids destruction of economic rents and expands our understanding of the implicit valuation of ecological goals.

Keywords: species viability, bycatch, fisheries management

The viability cost of carbon

Many climate scientists and policymakers have coalesced around a goal to limit the likelihood of a two degrees Celsius increase in average global temperature in the present century, compared to pre-industrial levels. This viability threshold is seen as an irreversible commitment to intensified warming (through feedback loops powered by effects like surface albedo). I aim to discuss the implicit value of this socially agreed upon physical climate target. A viability cost of carbon monetizes the motivation to take costly preventative action (and limit the probability of catastrophe); it captures the marginal social cost of violating this physical constraint. Like the social cost of carbon, this figure can be used in the cost-benefit analysis of various abatement programs.

Since the beginning of the modern climate change discussion, the focus has been to manage emissions to maximize a measure of expected welfare. However, the integrated assessment models that focus on this problem are commonly criticized for giving too much power to the modeler – power over arbitrary inputs such as “policy” parameters, damage functions, and the thickness of the tails of probability distributions – which influences results. My alternative provides a productive reaction to each of these complaints.

Keywords: viability cost of carbon, VCC, climate change, viable control

Exit behavior and the nature of trip taking in the South Alaska charter fishery

With Dan Lew and Jim Sanchirico

The recreational saltwater charter fishery in Alaska had undergone considerable growth over the past 30 years. Despite this, the extent to which the charter fishing sector has been impacted by harvest regulations, limited entry programs, and quota leasing systems has not been carefully analyzed. Using trip-level data of charter fishing effort and catch, we model the season-level decision of whether to exit or stay in the recreational charter halibut fishery and the within-season trip-taking behavior by recreational charter businesses, in response to these changes in the political seascape.

Keywords: charter fishing, entry-exit, policy evaluation

Limiting foresight in response to an ambiguous future

Natural resource and environmental management problems often involve planning over long time horizons amid many sources of risk (i.e. we know the distribution of future events) and ambiguity (which respects that we may not know the stochastic nature of a system beyond some local horizon). Instead of pretending to know the odds of future events, the resource manager I study prefers to shorten their planning horizon in deference to ambiguity. They continually concern themselves with this rolling window, full well knowing that they have some greater goal to be met at some point in the more-distant future. I believe this solution concept will be particularly useful in understanding managers concerned with dynamic systems that are expected to change over the near future due to climate change.

Keywords: bounded rationality, ambiguity aversion, limited foresight
